# Military Entrepreneurs and the Birth of Special Operations Forces

**Full title**: Military Entrepreneurs and the Birth of Special Operations Forces: Critical Junctures, Policy Streams, and Knowledge Diffusion in World War II

## Research question

Why did dozens of new, unconventional military units emerge in Allied armed forces between 1940 and 1943, despite deep institutional resistance to irregular warfare?

## Thesis / central argument

The formation of Allied special operations forces during World War II was not an inevitable institutional response to Axis advances but the product of entrepreneurial officers who exploited critical junctures — specifically the Fall of France (1940) and Pearl Harbor (1941) — when institutional shock temporarily suspended bureaucratic gatekeeping and opened policy windows for cost-effective, unconventional proposals. Cross-national knowledge diffusion from British to American military entrepreneurs then amplified these innovations, setting the structural foundations for the modern global SOF enterprise.

Three interlocking mechanisms:
1. **Critical junctures** (Capoccia & Kelemen, Collier & Collier, Soifer) — permissive conditions
2. **Kingdon's Multiple Streams** (Kingdon, Zahariadis) — coupling mechanism
3. **Knowledge diffusion** (Mintrom) — cross-national amplifier
