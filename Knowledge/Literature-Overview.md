# Literature Overview: Positioning the SOF Formation Argument

## The gap

Existing scholarship treats SOF formation as either:
1. A **functional response** to strategic need — units formed because they were needed (military innovation literature)
2. A product of **individual charisma** — great men willed units into existence (biographical/hagiographic tradition)

Neither is adequate. The functional account cannot explain *variation*: why some proposals were adopted and others rejected, why timing varied, why some units survived and others didn't. The great man account ignores the *structural conditions* that made entrepreneurship possible — without Dunkirk, Clarke's memo goes nowhere.

**This article integrates both** through a rigorous analytical framework: critical junctures provide the structural opening; military entrepreneurs provide the agency; knowledge diffusion provides the amplification mechanism.

## Key literatures to engage

### 1. Military innovation theory
- **Rosen (1991)**: *Winning the Next War* — innovation driven by senior military leaders promoting new theories of victory
- **Posen (1984)**: *Sources of Military Doctrine* — civilian intervention drives innovation; military organizations prefer offense
- **Murray (2011)**: *Military Adaptation in War* — wartime adaptation differs from peacetime innovation
- **Avant (1994)**: *Political Institutions and Military Change* — domestic political structure shapes military innovation
- **Grissom (2006)**: four models of military innovation (civil-military, interservice, intraservice, cultural)
- **Farrell & Terriff (2002)**: military change as social process

**ERC contribution**: Existing models focus on *organizational* innovation (doctrine, procurement, force structure). SOF formation is *entrepreneurial* innovation — individuals creating new organizational forms from below. The agent is the entrepreneur, not the institution.

### 2. Historical institutionalism — critical junctures
- **Capoccia & Kelemen (2007)**: canonical definition; distinguish contingency from determinism at junctures
- **Collier & Collier (1991)**: critical junctures and labor incorporation in Latin America — the template for juncture analysis
- **Soifer (2012)**: permissive vs. productive conditions — essential for separating structure from agency
- **Mahoney (2001)**: path dependence in macro-historical analysis

**Contribution**: Military historians use "turning point" loosely. This article applies the rigorous HI definition: a *brief period* when constraints relax and outcomes expand. Dunkirk and Pearl Harbor meet this definition precisely.

### 3. Public administration — Multiple Streams Analysis
- **Kingdon (1984/2010)**: *Agendas, Alternatives, and Public Policies* — the three-streams framework
- **Zahariadis (2003/2007)**: extends MSA to comparative and EU contexts
- **Mintrom (1997)**: policy entrepreneurs and diffusion of innovations across states
- **Mintrom & Norman (2009)**: policy entrepreneurship and policy change
- **Roberts & King (1991)**: policy entrepreneurs and radical policy change

**Contribution**: MSA has never been systematically applied to military organizational formation. The adaptation requires justification: wartime compresses windows, eliminates softening-up, makes the problem stream dominant. This is a genuine theoretical extension, not just application.

### 4. SOF-specific scholarship
- **Cohen (1978)**: *Commandos and Politicians* — the classic treatment of British Commando formation; emphasizes Churchill's role
- **King (2009, 2017)**: professionalization of Western military forces
- **Finlan (2008)**: SAS history and organizational development
- **Moreman**: British Commandos operational history
- **Dwyer**: Scouts and Raiders (UDT origins)

**Contribution**: SOF histories are rich in detail but theoretically thin. They describe *what happened* without explaining *why these proposals succeeded when others failed*. The three-streams framework provides the analytical mechanism.

### 5. Knowledge diffusion
- **Mintrom (1997)**: cross-state policy diffusion — the direct theoretical precedent
- **Berry & Berry (1990/2014)**: innovation and diffusion models in state politics
- **Simmons, Dobbin & Garrett (2006)**: international diffusion of policy

**Contribution**: Cross-national *military* diffusion is under-theorized. The UK → US SOF diffusion case is unusually clean: identifiable channels (Achnacarry, personnel rotation, institutional modeling), identifiable adaptations (Rangers ≠ Commandos), and a clear temporal lag (12–18 months).

## Positioning the argument

"This article argues that SOF formation was neither an inevitable functional response to strategic need nor the product of individual charisma alone, but the outcome of entrepreneurial officers who exploited critical junctures — moments when institutional shock temporarily suspended bureaucratic gatekeeping — to couple pre-existing unconventional ideas with newly exposed capability gaps and newly receptive senior patrons. Cross-national knowledge diffusion from British to American military entrepreneurs then amplified these innovations. By integrating historical institutionalism, Multiple Streams Analysis, and diffusion theory, the article provides a systematic framework for understanding military organizational innovation that moves beyond both structural determinism and voluntarist accounts."

## What the literature does NOT explain (the gap this paper fills)

1. **Why variation?** — Not all proposals succeeded; not all entrepreneurs created lasting organizations
2. **Why this timing?** — The 1940–43 cluster is not coincidental but structurally determined
3. **Why cross-national similarity?** — British and American SOF units resemble each other because of *diffusion*, not convergent evolution
4. **Why cost-effectiveness mattered** — Entrepreneurs succeeded because their proposals were cheap and fast, not because they were strategically optimal
5. **The agency-structure interaction** — Neither pure structure (junctures) nor pure agency (entrepreneurs) explains the outcome; only their interaction does
