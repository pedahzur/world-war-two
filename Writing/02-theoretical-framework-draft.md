# II. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

This article draws on three theoretical traditions — historical institutionalism, Multiple Streams Analysis (MSA), and policy diffusion theory — to explain the formation of Allied special operations forces during the Second World War. Each tradition addresses a distinct part of the puzzle. Historical institutionalism, and specifically its concept of "critical junctures," explains the structural conditions that made SOF formation possible: moments of institutional shock that temporarily suspended the bureaucratic gatekeeping that normally blocked unconventional proposals. Kingdon's Multiple Streams framework explains the mechanism through which entrepreneurial officers exploited those openings, coupling pre-existing ideas about irregular warfare with newly exposed capability gaps and newly receptive senior patrons. Policy diffusion theory explains the cross-national amplification of these innovations, as British organizational forms became the templates that American military entrepreneurs adapted to their own strategic contexts. Together, these three components form an integrated framework that accounts for structure, agency, and transmission — and that explains not only why certain proposals succeeded but also why others failed and why formation clustered within a narrow temporal window.

## A. Critical Junctures as Permissive Conditions

Military historians have long identified the Fall of France and the attack on Pearl Harbor as "turning points" in the war's trajectory. But the concept of a turning point, as conventionally deployed in historical narrative, lacks analytical precision. It identifies a moment when outcomes changed without specifying the institutional mechanisms through which change occurred. The concept of the "critical juncture," as developed within historical institutionalism, offers a more rigorous alternative.

Capoccia and Kelemen define critical junctures as "relatively short periods of time during which there is a substantially heightened probability that agents' choices will affect the outcome of interest."[^1] The defining feature is not the magnitude of the event itself but the degree to which it relaxes structural constraints on action: during a critical juncture, the range of plausible institutional outcomes expands dramatically, and choices that would be blocked or inconsequential under normal conditions acquire transformative potential. Collier and Collier's foundational study of labor incorporation in Latin America established the analytical template: critical junctures produce "legacies" — institutional arrangements that persist through self-reinforcing mechanisms long after the initial opening has closed.[^2] The organizational forms created during the 1940–1943 window — Commandos, SAS, SBS, Rangers, UDTs, OSS — became precisely such legacies, persisting as templates for every subsequent Western special operations force.

Soifer's refinement of the concept introduces a distinction that is essential for the present argument. He differentiates between *permissive conditions* — the structural changes that loosen constraints and widen the field of possible outcomes — and *productive conditions* — the agency that exploits the opening to produce a specific outcome.[^3] This distinction maps directly onto the empirical puzzle of SOF formation. The Fall of France and Dunkirk constituted the permissive condition for British SOF creation: they shattered prevailing strategic assumptions about continental warfare, eliminated Britain's conventional offensive capability, brought to power a prime minister personally disposed toward unconventional action, and created an institutional environment in which any proposal for offensive operations — however unorthodox — received a hearing that would have been unthinkable months earlier. Pearl Harbor served the same function for the United States, demolishing the assumption of strategic invulnerability and creating political space for organizational experimentation. But the permissive condition alone did not determine which specific organizational forms would emerge, or when. That required the productive condition: entrepreneurial officers who identified specific capability gaps, crafted proposals to fill them, and accessed senior patrons who could authorize their creation. Without the permissive condition, the entrepreneurs' proposals would have been blocked by routine bureaucratic gatekeeping. Without the productive condition — the entrepreneurs themselves — the permissive condition would have produced no specific organizational innovation.

This article also proposes an extension of the critical juncture concept that the empirical evidence demands: what I term "cascading junctures." The initial shock — Dunkirk, in the British case — did not simply open a single window for innovation. It generated secondary shocks that opened additional windows for different entrepreneurs at different moments. The disbandment of Layforce in 1941, itself a consequence of the initial strategic crisis, freed officers like David Stirling and Roger Courtney to propose entirely new organizational forms — the SAS and SBS, respectively — that would not have been possible had their parent unit survived. The fall of Singapore in February 1942 opened a distinct window in the Asian theater that Orde Wingate exploited to create the Chindits. The Tarawa disaster of November 1943, a consequence of the broader Pacific war unleashed by Pearl Harbor, created the specific sub-juncture that produced Underwater Demolition Teams. Each of these secondary shocks operated through the same mechanism as the primary juncture — relaxing constraints, expanding the range of possible outcomes, creating receptive patrons — but at different moments and in different theaters. The cascading structure of these junctures explains why SOF formation was not a single event but a temporally clustered sequence, with new units emerging over a roughly three-year period as successive shocks opened successive windows.

The critical juncture framework also generates a clear empirical prediction about timing: because junctures are by definition brief, the window for organizational creation should be temporally bounded. Units proposed within the window should have a high probability of approval; proposals advanced after the window closes, when bureaucratic routines have reconsolidated, should face the same resistance that blocked unconventional ideas before the juncture. The empirical record confirms this prediction. The overwhelming majority of British SOF formations occurred between June 1940 and mid-1942; American formations clustered between mid-1942 and late 1943. By 1944, as the Allies shifted to conventional offensive operations, the institutional environment had stabilized and the window for new SOF creation had largely closed.

## B. Multiple Streams Analysis as the Coupling Mechanism

If critical junctures explain *when* SOF formation became possible, Kingdon's Multiple Streams Analysis explains *how* specific proposals succeeded. Kingdon's framework, originally developed to explain agenda-setting and policy change in American domestic politics, posits that policy change occurs when three largely independent "streams" — problem recognition, policy proposals, and political receptivity — converge at a "policy window," coupled by the actions of a "policy entrepreneur."[^4] Each stream operates according to its own logic: problems gain attention through focusing events, indicators, or feedback; policy proposals develop in a "primeval soup" of ideas circulating among specialists; and the political stream shifts through changes in national mood, government composition, or interest group pressure. Policy change occurs not when any single stream demands it but when an entrepreneur successfully links a recognized problem to an available solution at a moment of political receptivity.

The application of MSA to military organizational formation requires adaptation, which this article undertakes explicitly rather than assuming a frictionless transfer. Three modifications are necessary.

First, the *problem stream* operates differently in wartime than in domestic politics. In Kingdon's original framework, problems compete for attention and may rise or fall on the agenda depending on how they are framed. In wartime, the problem stream is dominated by catastrophic military failure. The Fall of France, Pearl Harbor, the Tarawa reef disaster — these were not issues that needed to be "framed" for attention. They were visceral, unignorable capability gaps that demanded immediate response. The problem stream in wartime is not a contest for agenda space; it is an avalanche.

Second, the *policy stream* in the military domain consists not of proposals circulating among Washington policy communities but of pre-existing ideas about unconventional warfare that had been developed — often in marginal institutional spaces — before the crisis. Ralph Bagnold's deep desert navigation techniques were refined during civilian expeditions in the 1920s and 1930s. Colin Gubbins' guerrilla warfare manuals were written for MI(R) before the war. Evans Carlson's observations of Mao's guerrilla tactics dated from his time with the Chinese 8th Route Army in 1937–1938. Dudley Clarke's concept for raiding forces drew on his knowledge of Boer War commandos. These ideas existed in what Kingdon would recognize as a "primeval soup" — available but not yet viable, because no institutional opening existed for their implementation. The critical juncture made them viable by creating a demand for unconventional solutions that matched what these officers had already been thinking about.

Third, the *politics stream* in the military context is defined not by elections, national mood, or interest group pressure but by the receptivity of senior patrons — the generals, admirals, and political leaders who possessed the authority to approve the creation of new units. Churchill's personal enthusiasm for irregular warfare, Wavell's willingness to authorize "piracy" in the Western Desert, Auchinleck's openness to unorthodox proposals from junior officers, Marshall's readiness to adopt British innovations, Roosevelt's confidence in Donovan — these were the political conditions that enabled entrepreneurial proposals to succeed. The politics stream shifted because crisis made senior leaders receptive to ideas they would have dismissed in peacetime.

The concept of the "policy window" translates to the military domain with one critical modification: temporal compression. In Kingdon's domestic policy framework, entrepreneurs may spend years "softening up" policy communities, waiting for a window to open. In wartime, windows open suddenly through catastrophic failure and close rapidly as institutional routines reconsolidate or as the strategic situation changes. Dudley Clarke's proposal for Commandos went from memorandum to authorization in three days. Ralph Bagnold's pitch to Wavell for the Long Range Desert Group was approved in four days. David Stirling's proposal for the SAS was authorized within weeks. This compression means that the entrepreneur must already have a fully formed proposal — the policy stream must already contain the idea — when the window opens. There is no time for the incremental development that characterizes domestic policy entrepreneurship.

The coupling of streams is the entrepreneur's core function. The entrepreneur identifies a specific capability gap (problem stream), connects it to a pre-existing solution (policy stream), and presents it to a receptive patron (politics stream) at a moment when institutional constraints are relaxed (the policy window created by the critical juncture). When all three streams converge and an entrepreneur successfully couples them, the result is organizational creation. When any stream is absent — no identifiable capability gap, no viable solution, or no receptive patron — the proposal fails. This framework explains not only successful cases but also the proposals that were rejected or ignored: they lacked one or more of the three necessary conditions.

## C. Military Entrepreneurs as Policy Entrepreneurs

The figure who couples the three streams — the agent who transforms permissive conditions into productive outcomes — is the military entrepreneur. This article defines military entrepreneurs as officers who identify capability gaps exposed by institutional crisis, craft unconventional organizational proposals to fill them, access senior patrons who can authorize their implementation, and thereby create new military formations that would not have emerged through routine institutional processes.

The concept builds directly on the policy entrepreneurship literature. Mintrom defines policy entrepreneurs as individuals who seek to promote "significant policy change" by identifying problems, networking in policy circles, shaping the terms of policy debates, and building coalitions.[^5] Mintrom and Norman identify four critical activities: displaying social acuity (reading the institutional environment), defining problems (framing issues in ways that demand action), building teams (assembling the human capital for implementation), and leading by example (demonstrating commitment through personal risk).[^6] Roberts and King describe "policy entrepreneurs" who operate outside government to introduce, translate, and implement new ideas into public practice.[^7] Each of these characterizations maps onto the wartime military cases with striking fidelity.

The military entrepreneurs examined in this article engaged in four core activities that parallel the policy entrepreneurship literature:

*Problem identification and framing.* Each entrepreneur translated a broad strategic failure into a specific, solvable capability gap. Clarke did not propose a general response to Dunkirk; he proposed a specific type of raiding force modeled on Boer commandos. Bagnold did not propose a general response to Italy's entry into the war; he proposed a specific deep desert reconnaissance capability that exploited his unique navigational expertise. The framing mattered: proposals that identified a precise gap and offered a targeted solution succeeded; vague proposals for "unconventional warfare" did not.

*Patron access.* Each entrepreneur found a way to reach a senior decision-maker who could authorize unit creation — often bypassing the chain of command entirely. Stirling's physical scaling of the perimeter fence at Middle East HQ to reach Auchinleck is the most dramatic example, but every case involved some form of irregular access: Clarke's memo reached Churchill through Dill within twenty-four hours; Bagnold secured a direct meeting with Wavell; Donovan leveraged a personal relationship with Roosevelt that predated the war.

*Argument crafting.* Every successful proposal shared a common rhetorical structure: the proposed unit would be small, cheap, fast to assemble, and composed of existing personnel. No entrepreneur proposed a large, expensive formation that would compete with established units for resources. Cost-effectiveness was not incidental to the proposals; it was the feature that made them politically viable within the military bureaucracy. Senior patrons could approve them without triggering institutional resistance from existing formations.

*Cross-organizational networking.* The entrepreneurs operated across institutional boundaries — between services, between intelligence and operations, between national militaries. Donovan's prewar visits to British intelligence gave him the institutional template for the OSS. Truscott's assignment to Combined Operations Headquarters gave him direct exposure to the Commando model. Carlson's experience with Chinese guerrillas gave him a conceptual vocabulary that no American military institution could have provided.

This article further distinguishes between *formative entrepreneurs*, who create new organizational forms, and *transformative entrepreneurs*, who sustain, institutionalize, and adapt those forms over time. The empirical sections focus primarily on formative entrepreneurs — the founders — while noting that organizational survival beyond the initial window often required different skills and different agents.

## D. Knowledge Diffusion as Cross-National Amplifier

The final component of the framework addresses a pattern that is empirically striking but theoretically underdeveloped in the military innovation literature: the systematic diffusion of SOF organizational forms from Britain to the United States. Nearly every American SOF unit created during the war drew explicitly on British precedent. The Rangers trained at the British Commando depot at Achnacarry. The Marine Raiders' founding memorandum cited "British Commandos" as a model. The OSS was institutionally modeled on SOE, with British instructors training American operatives. Merrill's Marauders adopted the Chindit concept of long-range penetration. The temporal lag — American formations clustering twelve to eighteen months after their British equivalents — is consistent with a diffusion rather than independent invention explanation.

Mintrom's work on the diffusion of policy innovations across American states provides the theoretical foundation.[^8] He demonstrates that policy entrepreneurs in one jurisdiction adopt and adapt innovations pioneered in others, using the prior adoption as evidence of feasibility. The mechanism is not imitation but adaptation: entrepreneurs modify the imported model to fit local institutional conditions, strategic requirements, and available resources. This is precisely what American military entrepreneurs did. Carlson did not replicate British Commandos; he hybridized the Commando concept with Chinese guerrilla tactics to create something distinct. Underwater Demolition Teams developed capabilities that exceeded their British COPP predecessors because Pacific theater conditions — warm water, coral reefs, massive amphibious operations — demanded different solutions. The OSS developed an autonomous covert operations capability that SOE could not match due to the latter's colonial political constraints.

The diffusion operated through five identifiable channels: direct training exchange (Achnacarry), personnel rotation (British officers embedded in American training centers), institutional modeling (OSS structured on SOE), senior leader observation (Marshall's 1942 visit to Combined Operations Headquarters directly triggered the Ranger proposal), and doctrinal transfer (the Fairbairn-Sykes close combat system becoming standard across both British and American special operations and intelligence organizations). These channels constitute the mechanism through which British innovations entered the American policy stream, becoming the available solutions that American entrepreneurs coupled with American problems and American patrons.

In theoretical terms, cross-national knowledge diffusion functions as a second-order mechanism within the framework. The critical juncture and the three-streams coupling explain SOF formation within each national case. Diffusion explains the cross-national pattern: why American and British SOF units resembled each other, why the temporal lag was consistent, and why American entrepreneurs could move so quickly once their own juncture opened — they did not need to invent solutions from scratch because the British policy stream had already produced them.
